§ 01

Why Softmax fails under privacy

The motivation
Problem 01 · Training

LLMs memorize

Large language models trained on sensitive corpora can reproduce verbatim text from training data — exposing private information at inference time through membership inference and extraction attacks.

Problem 02 · Defence

DP-SGD is too costly

Per-sample gradient clipping during training scales poorly to billion-parameter models. Inference-time DP decoding — injecting noise into the token selection step — is far more practical, but has its own pitfalls.

Problem 03 · Mechanism

Softmax spreads the mass

The standard Exponential Mechanism assigns probability across all tokens. Under high privacy budgets, this assigns non-trivial mass to garbage tokens — producing incoherent text.

§ 02

Our solution

Replace Gumbel with Exponential

Report-Noisy-Max with Exponential Noise

Instead of adding Gumbel noise (which corresponds to Softmax sampling), we add i.i.d. Exponential noise to each logit and take the argmax. This is the Report-Noisy-Max (RNM) mechanism.

Exponential noise has a sharper one-sided decay than Gumbel. It strongly concentrates the winner on the highest-scoring tokens — exactly what we need to fight privacy-induced flattening.

yₜ = argmaxⱼ { ϕ̄ⱼ + Zⱼ }, Zⱼ ~ Exp(λ)
where λ = 2Δ/ε = 4C/(B·ε)
RNMExponential Noiseε-DPDClip sensitivityInvisibleInk

Building on InvisibleInk

We directly extend the InvisibleInk framework by Vinod et al. (2025), which achieves practical inference-time DP through two key ideas:

DClip — clips only the difference between private and public logits, reducing sensitivity to Δ = 2C/B while preserving distributional structure.

Top-k⁺ decoding — restricts sampling to a safe subset derived from public logits, avoiding unreliable vocabulary tail.

We keep both innovations unchanged and swap only the final sampling step: Gumbel-Max → RNM-Exponential. Same ε. Strictly better selection.

§ 04

Mathematical foundation

Three theorems that support us
Theorem 1 · Equivalence

RNM-Exp ≡ Permute-and-Flip

Ding et al. (2021) proved that adding i.i.d. Exponential noise to scores and taking the argmax produces exactly the same output distribution as the Permute-and-Flip mechanism by McKenna & Sheldon.

argmaxᵢ (qᵢ + Exp(λ)) ~ PF(q)

This lets us implement the simple RNM rule while inheriting all of PF's theoretical utility guarantees — including its known dominance over the Exponential Mechanism.

Theorem 2 · Privacy

Strict ε-DP under DClip

With DClip sensitivity Δ = 2C/B, calibrating the Exponential noise scale to λ = 2Δ/ε guarantees pure ε-differential privacy.

λ = 4C / (B · ε)

The proof follows a change-of-variables argument on the RNM probability integral, showing the likelihood ratio across adjacent datasets is bounded by eᵉ.

Theorem 3 · Utility

PF dominates EM — up to 2× better

For any privacy budget ε and any score distribution, the expected error of PF/RNM-Exp is always ≤ the expected error of the Exponential Mechanism (Softmax).

E[E_PF] ≤ E[E_EM] ∀ ε, q

In the asymptotic limit (gap between head and tail → ∞), the error ratio converges exactly to 2 — PF is twice as accurate as EM under the same privacy budget.

Equivalence confirmed

Softmax ≡ Gumbel-Max. RNM-Exp ≡ Permute-and-Flip. Both distributions match perfectly across 20,000 trials.

Utility advantage

PF/RNM consistently selects higher-scoring tokens for the same ε. The gap widens as privacy increases (ε → 0).

Tail suppression

Softmax assigns persistent probability to tail tokens. PF aggressively truncates the tail — low-ranked tokens approach zero selection probability.

2× error reduction

Across all simulated vocabulary sizes and p values, PF error ≤ EM error. The ratio converges to exactly 2 as p → 0, validating Theorem 3.
